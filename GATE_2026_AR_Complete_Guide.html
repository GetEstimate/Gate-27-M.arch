Architectural Graphics

Architectural graphics is the language through which architects communicate design intent. It includes a comprehensive set of drawing types — each serving a specific purpose in conveying spatial information at different scales and levels of abstraction.

Orthographic Projections are the primary documentation drawings. A plan is a horizontal section typically cut at 900–1000mm above the finished floor level, showing the arrangement of walls, openings, stairs, and furniture. An elevation is a vertical orthographic projection showing the external face of a building without foreshortening. A section is a vertical cut through the building revealing internal heights, floor-to-floor dimensions, ceiling types, foundations, and structural elements.

Axonometric and Isometric drawings show three faces of an object simultaneously without perspective distortion. In isometric, all three axes are at 120° to each other and drawn to true scale. In axonometric (plan oblique), the plan is drawn true-shape and extruded vertically.

Perspective drawings simulate the human eye view. In one-point perspective, one vanishing point on the horizon represents depth — ideal for interior corridors and streets. Two-point perspective has two vanishing points, ideal for building exteriors at corners. Three-point perspective adds a vertical vanishing point — used for dramatic aerial or worm's-eye views of tall buildings.

📐 Scale Formulas
Drawing Size = Actual Size ÷ Scale Denominator
Actual Size = Drawing Size × Scale Denominator
Representative Fraction (RF) = 1 : N (e.g. 1:100 means 1 unit = 100 units real)

Visual Composition in 2D and 3D

Visual composition governs how elements are organized to produce aesthetic order, meaning, and spatial quality. These principles apply equally to two-dimensional graphic design and three-dimensional architectural form.

  • Balance: Symmetrical balance (mirror image about an axis — e.g. Taj Mahal) vs Asymmetrical balance (different elements achieving equilibrium through placement — e.g. Fallingwater by Frank Lloyd Wright).
  • Rhythm: Repetition of similar elements at regular intervals (columnar rhythm of Greek temples), alternating rhythm (ABAB), progressive rhythm (escalating scale), and flowing rhythm (organic curves).
  • Proportion: The mathematical relationship between parts. The Golden Ratio (φ = 1.618) has been used since ancient Greece. The Modulor by Le Corbusier applied the Golden Section to human body measurements for architectural proportion.
  • Scale: Human scale (elements relate to human body size), monumental scale (deliberately oversized for grandeur — government buildings, religious structures), intimate scale (smaller than expected — creates coziness).
  • Hierarchy: Visual dominance achieved through size, color, position, or contrast — signals importance and organizes a composition.
  • Unity and Variety: Unity brings coherence; variety prevents monotony. The tension between these is the essence of good design.
  • Contrast: Juxtaposition of unlike elements — light vs dark, smooth vs rough, tall vs low — to create visual interest and emphasis.

Organization of Space

Spatial organization defines the structural logic of a building's plan. Francis Ching (Architecture: Form, Space & Order) identifies five fundamental spatial relationships and several organizational types:

Space within a space: A smaller space enclosed within a larger one. The inner space is the focus (e.g., altar within a cathedral nave).

Interlocking spaces: Two spaces overlap and share a common zone — the overlapping area belongs to both (e.g., living room and dining room sharing a central space).

Adjacent spaces: Two spaces share a common border. Relationship defined by the character of the shared boundary (wall, colonnade, change in level).

Spaces linked by a common space: Two spaces related by a third intermediary space — an entrance hall linking reception and office.

  • Linear organization: Sequence of spaces along a path (hospital corridor, railway carriage). Directional and hierarchical. Examples: Hypostyle hall at Karnak.
  • Radial organization: Spaces radiate from a dominant central space. Central point has visual and physical control (e.g., Pantheon, Bramante's Tempietto).
  • Clustered organization: Spaces grouped by proximity, shared form, or visual relationship without strong axis. Flexible, organic (village settlements, informal campus layouts).
  • Grid organization: Spaces organized on a regular 2D or 3D framework. Consistent, repetitive, easily expandable. Examples: Roman city grid (Timgad), Chandigarh sectors.
  • Axial organization: Spaces arranged along one or more dominant axes. Strong directionality and formality. Examples: Versailles gardens, Lutyens' New Delhi.
Circulation — Horizontal and Vertical

Horizontal Circulation: Movement along the same floor level — corridors, galleries, arcades, ramps, and passages. The configuration of horizontal circulation determines how spaces relate to each other and how efficiently people move through a building.

  • Single-loaded corridor: rooms on one side only — good daylighting to rooms, inefficient use of corridor area.
  • Double-loaded corridor: rooms on both sides — more efficient but interior-facing rooms get no direct light (used in hospitals, hotels, offices).
  • Gallery: wide corridor that is itself a usable space (art galleries, shopping arcades).
  • Enfilade: sequence of rooms opening directly onto each other — used in formal palaces (Versailles).

Vertical Circulation: Movement between floors — stairs, ramps, escalators, lifts, and fire escapes.

Universal Design

Universal Design, developed by Ron Mace at NC State University (1997), is the design of products and environments usable by all people, to the greatest extent possible, without adaptation or specialized design. It differs from "accessible design" (which only addresses disability) by aiming for inclusivity for all — children, elderly, pregnant women, people with temporary injuries, and people with disabilities.

The 7 Principles of Universal Design:

  1. Equitable Use: Useful to people with diverse abilities. The same entrance serves everyone — no separate "accessible" door. Example: automatic sliding doors at airports.
  2. Flexibility in Use: Accommodates a wide range of preferences and abilities. Example: scissors usable by left or right-handed people; adjustable-height desks.
  3. Simple and Intuitive Use: Easy to understand regardless of experience, language, or cognitive ability. Example: clear pictograms, progressive color coding in wayfinding.
  4. Perceptible Information: Communicates necessary information effectively using different modes — tactile paving (visual + tactile), audio signals at crossings, Braille signage.
  5. Tolerance for Error: Minimizes hazards and adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions. Example: rounded furniture corners, guarded stovetop controls.
  6. Low Physical Effort: Efficient and comfortable with minimum fatigue. Example: lever door handles instead of round knobs; touch-sensitive faucets.
  7. Size and Space for Approach and Use: Appropriate size and space provided regardless of user's body size, posture, or mobility. Example: knee clearance at counters, 1500mm turning radius for wheelchairs.
GATE Key Points: Tactile paving (yellow, 300mm wide) must be provided along accessible routes. The international symbol of accessibility (wheelchair pictogram) was designed by Susanne Koefoed in 1968, revised 2012. Accessibility standards in India: CPWD Handbook on Barrier Free and Accessible Environment (2014).

PERT and CPM — Project Management Techniques

CPM (Critical Path Method) was developed in 1957 by DuPont for industrial projects. It uses deterministic (fixed) time estimates for each activity and identifies the critical path — the longest path through the network, which determines the minimum project duration. Any delay on the critical path delays the entire project.

PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique) was developed in 1958 by the US Navy for the Polaris missile project. It uses three probabilistic time estimates per activity and is better for projects with high uncertainty (R&D, new construction types).

📐 PERT Formulae
Expected time (te) = (to + 4·tm + tp) / 6
Variance (σ²) = [(tp − to) / 6]²
Standard deviation (σ) = (tp − to) / 6
where: to = optimistic time, tm = most likely time, tp = pessimistic time
Total Float (TF) = LS − ES = LF − EF
Free Float (FF) = ES(successor) − EF(activity)
Independent Float (IF) = ES(successor) − LF(predecessor) − Duration
Critical Path: Activities where TF = 0
📊 PERT Worked Example

Activity A: to=2, tm=5, tp=14 days

te = (2 + 4×5 + 14)/6 = (2 + 20 + 14)/6 = 36/6 = 6 days

σ = (14 − 2)/6 = 12/6 = 2 days

σ² = 4 days²

CPM Float Example: Activity X has ES=5, EF=9 (duration 4 days). Its successor starts at ES=12 (LS=15).

Total Float = 15 − 5 = 10 days (can delay by 10 days without delaying project)
Free Float = 12 − 9 = 3 days (can delay by 3 days without delaying successor)

Estimation and Specification

Estimation involves calculating the cost of a construction project. Specifications define the quality standards for materials and workmanship. Together they form the basis of tendering and contract documentation.

Types of Estimates:

  • Preliminary/Approximate Estimate: Quick cost assessment at concept stage. Methods: plinth area method, cubic content method, bay method. Accuracy ±20–30%.
  • Detailed/Revised Estimate: Based on working drawings. Uses Bill of Quantities (BOQ). Accuracy ±5–10%.
  • Supplementary Estimate: Additional works not in original scope.
  • Annual Maintenance Estimate: For ongoing repair and upkeep.
📐 Estimation Methods
Plinth Area Cost = Plinth Area (m²) × Plinth Area Rate (₹/m²)
Cubical Content Cost = Volume (m³) × Cubic Rate (₹/m³)
BOQ Method: Cost of item = Quantity × Rate per unit
Rate Analysis: Unit Rate = Material cost + Labour cost + Overhead (10–15%) + Profit (10–15%)
Abstract Estimate = Σ (Quantity × Rate) for all items

Disaster-Resistant Structures

India is highly vulnerable to natural hazards. About 59% of land is earthquake-prone, 68% of cultivable land is drought-prone, 75% of coastline is cyclone-prone, and ~40 million hectares is flood-prone. The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) provides codes for disaster-resistant design.

Seismic Design (IS:1893-2016): India is divided into 4 seismic zones (II, III, IV, V — lowest to highest hazard). The seismic design force is calculated as a product of the seismic zone factor, importance factor, response reduction factor, and the design base shear formula.

Ecological Principles

Ecology studies the relationships between organisms and their environment. For architects and planners, ecological principles guide sustainable design decisions — from site selection to material choice to urban form.

  • Ecosystem: A community of living organisms interacting with their physical environment (soil, water, air, energy). Example: a wetland ecosystem includes water, aquatic plants, birds, fish, and microorganisms.
  • Food web and energy flow: Energy flows from producers (plants) to consumers (herbivores → carnivores) with approximately 10% efficiency at each trophic level (Lindemann's 10% rule).
  • Carrying Capacity (K): The maximum population an ecosystem can sustainably support. When population exceeds K, resources deplete and population crashes.
  • Ecological Footprint: The land area needed to sustain a population's resource consumption and absorb its waste. The global average is ~2.8 ha/person; Earth can sustainably provide ~1.6 ha/person — indicating global overshoot.
  • Urban Heat Island (UHI): Urban areas are 2–5°C warmer than surrounding rural areas due to dark impervious surfaces, reduced vegetation, waste heat from buildings and vehicles. Mitigation: green roofs, urban forests, cool pavements, water bodies.
📐 Ecological Footprint
EF = (Population × Per capita consumption) / Productivity per hectare
Biocapacity Reserve = Biocapacity − Ecological Footprint (positive = surplus)
Energy at each trophic level = 10% of previous level (Lindemann's Law)
Environmental Pollution — Types, Causes, Controls

Air Pollution: Pollutants include Particulate Matter (PM2.5, PM10), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂), Carbon Monoxide (CO), Carbon Dioxide (CO₂), Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs), and Ozone (O₃). Sources: vehicles, industry, construction, biomass burning. India's National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) set permissible limits.

Water Pollution: Measured by Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), Total Dissolved Solids (TDS), and coliform count. BOD is the key indicator for organic pollution — clean water has BOD < 1 mg/L; heavily polluted water has BOD > 10 mg/L.

Noise Pollution: Measured in decibels (dB). The dB scale is logarithmic — 10 dB increase = 10× increase in intensity. Every doubling of distance reduces noise by 6 dB for a point source.

Concepts and Theories of Urban Design

Urban design operates at the intersection of architecture, planning, and landscape — shaping the public realm and the physical environment of cities at the scale between individual buildings and the city as a whole.

  • Kevin Lynch — Image of the City (1960): Cities are perceived through 5 elements: Paths (routes of movement — streets, walkways), Edges (boundaries and linear breaks — walls, rivers, rail lines), Districts (areas with distinct character perceived as having identity), Nodes (strategic points of concentration — intersections, squares), and Landmarks (external point references — towers, monuments). Imageability is the quality of a place that evokes a strong mental image.
  • Gordon Cullen — Townscape (1961): Serial vision (experience of moving through a sequence of spaces), Optics (here/there — visual contrast between existing and emerging views), Place (sense of belonging — position, territory), Content (fabric, colour, texture, scale).
  • Jane Jacobs — Death and Life of Great American Cities (1961): Conditions for vibrant city streets: mixed primary uses, short blocks, aged buildings, concentration of people. Eyes on the street = natural surveillance = safety.
  • Jan Gehl — Cities for People (2010): 12 quality criteria for public spaces in 3 categories: Protection (from traffic, crime, sensory unpleasantness), Comfort (walking, standing, sitting, seeing, talking, playing), Delight (scale, weather, positive sensory experiences).
  • New Urbanism: Charter of New Urbanism (1993). Walkable neighbourhoods, mixed uses, traditional architecture, transit connections, human scale. Design principles: interconnected street grid, narrow streets, front porches, garages at rear.
  • Leon Krier: The city of quarters — urban blocks with perimeter buildings defining streets and squares. Critique of functional zoning; advocates mixed-use urban fabric.
Development Controls — FAR, Densities, Byelaws

Development controls are the regulatory tools that shape how the built environment is formed. They operate at the plot level (setbacks, coverage, FAR) and the urban level (zoning, density controls, design guidelines).

Heritage Conservation

Heritage conservation protects culturally significant buildings, precincts, and landscapes for future generations. Conservation philosophy is guided by international charters (Venice Charter 1964, Burra Charter 1979) and national legislation (Ancient Monuments Act 1958).

Degrees of Intervention (least to most invasive):

  1. Preservation: Maintaining existing form without change; arresting decay.
  2. Conservation: Maintaining and/or repairing existing fabric.
  3. Consolidation: Binding together components to maintain structural integrity.
  4. Restoration: Returning to a known earlier state (only when evidence exists).
  5. Rehabilitation/Adaptive Reuse: Enabling compatible contemporary use while retaining cultural significance.
  6. Reconstruction: Re-creating a vanished structure (requires substantial evidence).

India's Heritage Grading (ASI + Local Bodies):

  • Grade I: Exceptional national/international importance (ASI-protected). No alteration, additions, or demolition permitted.
  • Grade II (A & B): Important regional monuments. Limited alterations with expert committee approval.
  • Grade III: Local significance. Alterations permitted with guidelines.
Key Facts: UNESCO has 42 World Heritage Sites in India (as of 2023). INTACH (Indian National Trust for Art and Cultural Heritage) was founded in 1984. Venice Charter (1964): "The intention in conserving and restoring monuments is to safeguard them no less as works of art than as historical evidence." Buffer zone around ASI monuments: 100m (prohibited) + 200m (regulated).

Neighbourhood Unit (Perry) and Affordable Housing

Perry's Neighbourhood Unit (1929) is the foundational concept in residential planning. It creates a self-sufficient residential area scaled to elementary school attendance — defining the ideal walking distance for children and residents.

6 Principles of Perry's Neighbourhood Unit:

  1. Size: Should house as many families as needed for one elementary school (~5,000–10,000 people; area ~65 ha; radius ~400–800m).
  2. Boundaries: Bounded by arterial roads on all sides, not crossed by through traffic.
  3. Open Spaces: System of small parks and recreation spaces (~10% of total area).
  4. Institution Sites: School and other civic buildings at the centre or in common areas.
  5. Local Shops: Located at periphery at traffic junctions, not inside the residential area.
  6. Internal Street System: Internal streets designed for local circulation only — reduces through traffic, ensures safety.

Solar Architecture and Thermal Design

Solar angles: Understanding sun path is fundamental to passive solar design. The sun's altitude (height above horizon) and azimuth (compass bearing) determine when, where, and how much solar radiation strikes a building surface.

📐 Solar Geometry Formulas
Declination angle: δ = 23.45 × sin[360/365 × (284 + n)] n = day of year
Hour angle: h = 15 × (solar time − 12) (degrees)
Solar altitude: sin α = sin φ sin δ + cos φ cos δ cos h
where φ = latitude, δ = declination, h = hour angle
Solar azimuth: cos A = (sin δ − sin φ sin α) / (cos φ cos α)
Shade required: overhang projection = window height / tan(altitude angle at noon, summer)
Solar insolation on tilted surface: I_t = I_b × cos θ + I_d + I_r

Q: At Nagpur (φ=21°N), on June 21 (δ=+23.45°), find noon solar altitude and required overhang for a 1.5m tall south window.

= 0.358 × 0.398 + 0.934 × 0.917

sin α = sin21°×sin(−23.45°) + cos21°×cos(−23.45°)×1 = −0.142 + 0.856 = 0.714
α = 45.5° — sun enters well below overhang — winter solar gain achieved ✓

Central Place Theory (Christaller)

Walter Christaller (1933) developed Central Place Theory to explain the spatial distribution, size, and number of towns. A central place provides goods and services to its surrounding hinterland. The key concepts are: range (maximum distance consumers travel for a good), threshold (minimum population needed to sustain a service), and hexagonal market areas (the most efficient non-overlapping service areas).

📐 Christaller's K-Values
K = 3 (Marketing Principle): each lower-order centre serves K-1 hinterlands
K = 4 (Transport Principle): settlements maximize road access efficiency
K = 7 (Administrative Principle): lower-order centres wholly within higher-order hinterlands
Number of settlements at each level (K=3): 1 → 2 → 6 → 18 → 54 (×3 pattern)
Gravity Model: Interaction I_ij = k × (P_i × P_j) / d_ij²
where P = population of cities i,j; d = distance; k = constant
Reilly's Law of Retail Gravitation: Break point = d_ij / (1 + √(P_j/P_i))
📊 Gravity Model Example

Q: City A (pop=1 million) and City B (pop=250,000) are 120 km apart. Where is the retail break point (equal drawing power) from City A?

Break point from A = 120 / (1 + √(250,000/1,000,000))
= 120 / (1 + √0.25)
= 120 / (1 + 0.5)
= 120 / 1.5 = 80 km from City A (40 km from City B)

📐 Population Projection Formulas
1. Arithmetic (Linear): Pn = Po + n × r [r = mean annual increment]
2. Geometric (Exponential): Pn = Po × (1 + i)^n [i = growth rate/year]

Four-Step Travel Demand Model

The four-step model is the standard framework for transportation demand forecasting. It converts land use data into future traffic volumes on a road network.

  1. Trip Generation: Estimates the number of trips produced by and attracted to each traffic analysis zone (TAZ). Uses regression models or trip rate tables based on land use, density, car ownership, income.
    • Production model: Trips = f(households, income, car ownership)
    • Attraction model: Trips = f(employment, floor space, retail area)
  2. Trip Distribution: Determines where trips go — connecting production zones to attraction zones. The Gravity Model is most commonly used.
  3. Modal Split: Determines what mode of transport is used — private car, bus, metro, walking, cycling. Uses logit choice models based on travel time, cost, comfort, frequency.
  4. Traffic Assignment: Loads trips onto the road/transit network. Common methods: All-or-Nothing (AON), User Equilibrium (Wardrop's 1st principle), System Optimum (Wardrop's 2nd principle).
📐 Transportation Formulas
Gravity model: T_ij = P_i × [A_j × F_ij] / Σ(A_j × F_ij)
where F_ij = friction factor = k / t_ij^n (t=travel time, n=2 typically)
Logit modal split: P(mode k) = e^(U_k) / Σe^(U_m)
Utility function: U = a + b₁t + b₂c + ... (t=time, c=cost, b=coefficients)
Wardrop's 1st principle: In equilibrium, no traveller can reduce travel cost by switching routes
Travel demand forecasting: V_future = V_present × (Growth factor)^n
Road Capacity and Level of Service
📐 Capacity and LOS Formulas (IRC/HCM)
Capacity at signalized intersection: C = S × g/C_cycle
S = saturation flow = 1900 × PCE/hour/lane (for ideal conditions)
Degree of saturation (x) = q / c (q = flow, c = capacity)
Webster's optimum cycle length: C_o = (1.5L + 5) / (1 − Y)
where L = total lost time/cycle, Y = Σ(y_i) = sum of critical flow ratios
Green time: g_i = (C_o − L) × y_i / Y
Average delay (Webster): d = C(1−g/C)² / [2(1−xg/C)] + x² / [2q(1−x)]
PCU conversion: Car=1, 2-wheeler=0.5, Auto=0.75, Bus=3.0, Truck=3.0, Cycle=0.2

Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS):

  • Adaptive Traffic Control (SCATS/SCOOT): Adjusts signal timings in real-time based on loop detector/camera feeds. Reduces delays by 15–25% vs fixed-time signals.
  • ATMS (Advanced Traffic Management System): Central control room monitoring cameras, VMS (Variable Message Signs), ramp metering, incident detection.
  • AVMS (Automatic Vehicle Monitoring System): GPS tracking of buses; feeds real-time info to GTFS (General Transit Feed Specification) for passenger apps.
  • Electronic Toll Collection (ETC): FASTag (India) uses RFID transponders; reduces toll plaza delays from 5–10 min to <5 sec.
  • Congestion Pricing: Dynamic road pricing to manage peak-hour demand. Example: London Congestion Charge (£15/day, 2022), Singapore's ERP (Electronic Road Pricing — world's first, 1998).
